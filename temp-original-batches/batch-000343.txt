Make Mucenici
Mucenici is a traditional Romanian pastry prepared for the Christian feast of the 40 Martyrs of Sebaste on March 9th. They are created by rolling and shaping dough into figure eights, which represents eternity and remembrance of the soldiers who were persecuted. The pastries embody the cultural heritage of those who observe the day and celebrate culinary tradition, bringing families together to honor the past and welcome spring.\n\nThe preparation method of mucenici varies depending on the region. In Moldova, the famous Moldavian mucenici are made from sweet bread dough. They are baked in the oven, then coated with honey and sprinkled with walnuts. In Muntenia, Oltenia, and Dobrogea, the dough for mucenici is much simpler, with only three ingredients. These are boiled in water with sugar, walnuts, and cinnamon—the water symbolizing the lake where the soldiers were thrown. Although the preparation method differs, their shape has remained unchanged—the figure 8. The enjoyment of mucenici is a communal activity that strengthens family bonds and cultural identity, making it a cherished part of Romanian heritage.

Cook Matzo Ball Soup
Matzo Ball Soup isn't just a staple at many Pesach celebrations; it's also the food your bubbe or zayde might make for you when you're feeling a little under the weather as a cure-all.\n\nThe recipe for matzo ball soup has roots in Ashkenazi Jewish cooking in Eastern Europe and is a type of chicken soup with large dumplings made of matzah meal and eggs; the broth has what's known as schmaltz (chicken fat). Matzah on its own is a flatbread served at Pesach, when Jews refrain from eating leavened bread for the duration of the holiday as of a celebration of their escape from enslavement in Egypt.\n\nHistorically, the matzo balls have actually been called knoedel (or knaidel, or kneidel... exact spelling varies) instead of matzo. Now you can enjoy a soup that's as nourishing for the spirit and soul as it is for the body.\n\nLactose Free

Make Mămăligă
Mămăligă is a cherished traditional dish found in many Eastern European countries, and some hail it as the national dish of Romania. Originally made with millet flour, today it is a polenta crafted from yellow maize flour. It can be prepared to various consistencies, serving as a comforting porridge or a delightful bread substitute.\n\nMămăligă is incredibly versatile and pairs well with cheese and other toppings. Our own recipe includes cottage cheese, tomatoes, and basil. Rich in history and flavor, mămăligă continues to be a staple in Eastern European cuisine, celebrated for its simplicity and adaptability.
